## Theories of Consciousness

**Consciousness as experience and awareness.** Much cognitive processing is not conscious, yet experience itself seems inseparable from consciousness. Although the term can also refer to a level of arousal, being awake rather than asleep, the central issue is consciousness as experience itself.

**Phenomenal consciousness.** This is the subjective, qualitative aspect of experience: what it feels like to have a given experience. Explaining phenomenal consciousness would require understanding how physical matter gives rise to subjective experience. So far, attempts to bridge what Joseph Levine called the *explanatory gap* in 1983 have failed. This enduring puzzle, the link between matter and subjective experience, is also what David Chalmers called the *hard problem* of consciousness in 1996.

**Conscious access.** This is the aspect of consciousness that makes information explicitly available for cognitive processing and behavioral control. The simplest marker is reportability: if a subject can verbally report it, it becomes scientifically investigable. If phenomenal consciousness is about what experience is like, conscious access is about how experience becomes usable. This is the aspect most directly studied in science, perhaps in the hope that it may eventually illuminate phenomenal consciousness.

Reference: *Theories of Consciousness* — Anil K. Seth and Tim Bayne, 2022.

For present purposes, what matters is the broad structure of the debate. Four major families of theory are especially relevant:

1. **Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT):** consciousness arises when information is globally broadcast across the brain, enabling communication, control, access, and integration.
2. **Integrated Information Theory (IIT):** consciousness corresponds to the maximization of integrated information, $\Phi$, within a system.
3. **Higher-Order Theory (HOT):** conscious states are states represented by higher-order thoughts, involving reflexivity and second-order representation.
4. **Reentry and Predictive Processing (PP):** consciousness is tied to feedback loops within a hierarchical predictive system that continuously minimizes prediction error.

## Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT)

> Main proponents: GNWT was proposed by Stanislas Dehaene and Lionel Naccache, and develops earlier work by Bernard J. Baars.

### Key Idea

Consciousness arises when information is globally broadcast through a network of long-distance neuronal connections, the *workspace*, enabling access and coordination among specialized brain modules.

The key reference here is *Towards a Cognitive Neuroscience of Consciousness: Basic Evidence and a Workspace Framework* — Dehaene and Naccache, 2001.

### Starting Point: Fundamental Findings About Consciousness

The theory begins from three basic findings:

1. accurate processing is possible without consciousness, as in patients detecting objects in a blind visual field;
2. attention is a prerequisite for consciousness, amplifying or damping stimuli so they may or may not enter awareness;
3. consciousness is required for some tasks but not others, especially maintaining information in memory, solving novel problems, and generating intentional behavior without explicit instruction.

### Theoretical Architecture

The theory relies on three core ideas:

1. unconscious processes are modular and rely on specialized brain systems;
2. conscious processes arise from the interconnection and integration of many such specialized modules;
3. this interconnection is driven by attentional amplification of relevant signals and by the dynamic mobilization of relevant modules.

### Mechanism of Access

Conscious access depends on a distributed neural system called the *global workspace*, which has long-distance connectivity linking multiple specialized modules. The workspace coordinates and integrates information exchanged with those modules.

**Top-down attentional amplification.** To enter the workspace and become conscious, a modular process must be amplified and sustained long enough to cross a threshold of activation.

**No homunculus required.** This mobilization does not need a supervising inner observer. It emerges from stochastic activity in workspace neurons, selected in relation to current context.

> This suggests an emergent and potentially self-organizing character of consciousness, where context helps determine what is selected for global broadcasting.

**Consciousness is not localized.** It is defined less by a fixed place in the brain than by a style of activation: dynamic, long-distance mobilization. Which modules participate in the workspace changes with the form of attention being deployed.

**Unity and reportability.** The apparently unitary character of consciousness, and its reportability, may be explained by the interconnection of perceptual, motor, long-term memory, evaluative, and attentional circuits involved in conscious episodes.

### Implications of the Theoretical Framework

The authors draw several implications from this model.

**Structural constraints.** For information encoded in the brain to become conscious:

1. it must be represented by neuronal firing;
2. it must stand in a bidirectional relation with the workspace, allowing a sustained amplification loop.

Representations that do not meet these conditions, for example hard-encoded ones, are not accessible to consciousness.

**Temporal granularity.** Consciousness is constrained by the time required to establish the amplification loop. Some ramp-up is needed before the workspace can sustain module activity and the modules can sustain workspace activity in return.

**Neural substrates.** Brain imaging work around 2001 suggested that specific brain regions are associated with particular conscious experiences. GNWT predicts that these regions should share a common access mechanism. The prefrontal cortex (PFC) and anterior cingulate (AC), both characterized by long-range connectivity, are candidates for that mechanism.

**The hard problem may dissolve.** The authors suggest that the hard problem could become less mysterious as our understanding of consciousness improves. They briefly extend this possibility to issues such as free will, phenomenal consciousness, and the sense of self.

### Further Implications

1. **Free will:** it is experienced when decision mechanisms involve the PFC or AC.
2. **Phenomenal and access consciousness:** they may share the same underlying nature and differ only in degree or processing level. In the theory, information may be inaccessible to consciousness, available to conscious processing, or fully broadcast within the workspace. The fully broadcast state corresponds to access consciousness, while the intuition of a richer, unprocessed field may correspond to phenomenal consciousness.
3. **Sense of self:** this arises when self-coding modules, bodily awareness, homeostasis, identity, personal history, self-modeling, are mobilized in the workspace and become available for inspection by other modules.
4. **Evolutionary perspective:** the theory can be seen as an extension of the increasingly rich internal representations developed by organisms, enhancing simulation and partially decoupling decision-making from immediate environmental feedback.
