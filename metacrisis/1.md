# A Journey Through John Vervaeke's Series: Awakening from the Meaning Crisis — 1

Aside from its engaging core focus, I found the series to provide an excellent scaffolding for further exploration of cognitive science. These notes are that scaffolding. While aiming to be faithful to the series, they also include personal thoughts and reflections, not always explicitly marked as such.

## All Is Fucked Up

*Episode 1: What Is the Meaning Crisis?*

"All is fucked-up" is one way the meaning crisis hits us. As the expression suggests, the world feels:

1. broken, damaged, or not working properly;
2. stupid and not reasonable;
3. emotionally damaging and deeply unsatisfying.

**Causes of the meaning crisis.** This internal machinery that makes us adaptive also makes us prone to self-deception and to bullshitting ourselves. When dysfunctional, it produces a loss of meaning in our lives.

**Effects of the meta-crisis.** These include nihilism (lack of meaning), cynicism (loss of faith in humans), and the pursuit of ways to regain meaning. Some of these are constructive, such as the re-emergence of stoicism and mindfulness; others are pathological, such as totalitarianism and selfish politics.

**Objectives of _Awakening from the Meaning Crisis_.** The series aims to establish:

1. an understanding of how Western culture built and lost its tools for meaning;
2. a scientific account of what meaning is and how cognitive science can explain it;
3. a starting point for navigating out of the meaning crisis.

## Shamanism, Insights, and Altered States of Consciousness

*Episode 1: Primitive Meaning-Making from the Distant Past*

**Shamanism as a way to connect.** Shamanism was pervasive in the Paleolithic era. It can be understood as a set of practices aimed at establishing connections within the group, with nature, and with the afterlife.

Reference: *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy* — Mircea Eliade and Wendy Doniger, 1951 (French), 1964 (English), 2020 (latest edition).

**Altered states of consciousness.** These are believed to have been a means for shamans to gain insight and healing power, hypothetically through mechanisms such as the placebo effect. Rituals and symbols played a major role in these altered states of consciousness, and they will return as central themes later.

**Relevance for today's meaning crisis.** We will explore how the following are essential to meaning in life:

1. a sense of connectedness;
2. the capacity for insight;
3. rituals and symbols;
4. altered states of consciousness.

All of these are already present in ancient shamanic practices.

This raises the question of whether our past might provide tools for meaning, and whether modern minds are capable of discerning what is valuable in those traditions without simply inheriting their superstitions.

## The Flow State: A Cascade of Insights

*Episode 2: A Present Echo of What We Seek*

**What is the flow state?** It is a sense of oneness, clarity, effortless action, and ego dissolution. It is produced by a surge in communication between brain regions, and it enhances metaphorical thinking and problem-solving capacity.

References:

- *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience* — Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, 1990.
- *The Evolving Self: A Psychology for the Third Millennium* — Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, 1993.

**How is it achieved?** Flow tends to be induced by:

1. clear goals;
2. immediate feedback;
3. skin in the game, where error matters.

Rock climbing is a good example of an environment conducive to the flow state.

**A cascade of insights.** This is a useful way to think about flow. Insights and the flow state can be understood as preliminary stages to altered states of consciousness, which were already part of shamanic practices.

## The Axial Revolution, or the Rise of Metacognition

*Episodes 2 and 3: The key capability of being able to think about how we think*

**The Bronze Age collapse as a trigger.** The emergence of the Axial Age around 1000 BCE followed the collapse of major civilizations such as Mycenaean Greece, the Kassites in Babylonia, the Hittite Empire in Anatolia, and the New Kingdom of Egypt. The slow recomposition that followed fostered innovations and new psychotechnologies, especially alphabetic literacy and money. Humans increasingly began to think about how they think.

Reference: *1177 B.C.: The Year Civilization Collapsed* — Eric Cline, 2015.

**Shamanism as a root of the Axial Age.** One can argue that shamanism participated in the exaptation of our cognitive capacities, eventually contributing to metacognition and second-order thinking: the ability to think about how we think.

> Exaptation is the evolutionary phenomenon whereby a trait that originally evolved for one purpose is later used for another.

**The axial revolution in Greece.** It is characterized by the emergence of transcendence over immanence. The horizontal worldview of Greek mythology, where individuals, nature, and gods lie on a continuum, is replaced by the vertical worldview of Greek philosophy.

**The axial revolution in the Middle East.** Through the Israelites' innovation of a monotheistic God, faith becomes a matter of reciprocal realization. Humans and God jointly unfold and realize themselves in history. The role of the prophets is to awaken God's people when they drift away from that historical task.

## Next Stop on the Journey

Both axial revolutions provide a scaffolding of coherence, significance, and purpose that helped sustain meaning within Western culture.

Questions that follow from this:

1. What was this scaffolding?
2. How was it lost?
3. Is there something worth saving from it and from these ancient traditions?
4. If so, how can it be integrated into a modern scientific worldview?

This journey through John Vervaeke's series explores those questions with the hope of finding some initial guidance out of the meaning crisis.

The next stop will turn to Greek thinking about meaning in life and make a first attempt at bridging it with ideas from psychology and the theory of complex dynamical systems.
