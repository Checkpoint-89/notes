## Meaning Seeking for Plato and Aristotle

*Episodes 4, 5, 6, and 7: Meaning requires us to resolve inner conflicts and connect with reality deeply enough to know it*

**Self-deception, ignorance, and suffering.** For the ancient Greeks, self-deception is fed by ignorance and leads to suffering.

> Self-deception can be understood as fooling oneself, consciously or not, in order to avoid facing reality.

Reason is central to overcoming self-deception, as shown by the Socratic method of critical inquiry, which challenges one's assumptions through rigorous questioning.

**Plato's two meta-desires.** Plato identifies two meta-desires underlying all other desires. They aim at escaping suffering by:

1. resolving our inner conflicts;
2. getting in touch with reality.

This is also what the flow state seems to achieve: deep integration within ourselves and with the world.

**Preventing inner conflict through character.** For Plato, inner conflict is resolved by teaching Logos (reason), taming Eros (emotion), and training Thymos (the social, status-seeking part of us). Aristotle extends this by emphasizing the development of virtues and character. The next section takes up that issue more directly.

**Getting in touch with reality through anagoge.** In Plato's myth of the cave, one gets closer to reality through a process of self-realization. It dispels the shadows that cover the true self and obscure vision. This process, called *anagoge*, is described through the ascent from the cave's depths to the surface. That ascent transforms the individual, which transforms the worldview, which in turn transforms the individual again. Anagoge is therefore a process of increased participatory knowing between oneself and the world.

**Getting in touch with reality by grasping it more deeply.** Aristotle's con-formity theory can be illustrated through the craftsman. Through practice and experience, the craftsman has been informed by the form of the object he makes. He knows the object by being able to conform to it: practice gives him an intuitive grasp of the thing itself. This knowledge is partly ineffable, because grasping reality in that way differs from propositional knowledge.

Reference: *The Tacit Dimension* — Michael Polanyi, 1966 (first edition), 2009 (later edition).

> "We can know more than we can tell."

## Resolving Inner Conflict: Cultivation of Character, Living Organisms, and Dynamical Systems

*Episode 6: We grow our character and resolve inner conflict by setting up the right internal dynamical systems*

**Actualizing potential.** Aristotle is helpful for understanding personal growth through his concept of actualizing potential. A piece of wood has the potential to become a table, and the carpenter actualizes that potential by exerting constraints over it. Living organisms are more remarkable still: they can self-actualize, realizing their potential through the constraints they adopt.

**Against a purely linear view of causality.** Aristotle's understanding of change was later challenged by the linear Newtonian view of causality. The idea that every effect has a cause appears intuitive and powerful, but it becomes inadequate when applied to biology.

Reference: *The Entangled Brain* — Luiz Pessoa, 2022.

> "A central thesis of the book is that biology does not work like physics, and even less so like engineering."

The causal view helps us avoid circular explanations, but it becomes perplexing with living organisms, which operate through intrinsic feedback loops: a tree causes the growth of its leaves, which in turn sustain the tree. This issue has been discussed at least since Kant's reflections on teleology.

**Constraints and dynamical systems.** Returning to the Aristotelian inspiration, emphasizing constraints over effects shifts attention from what actually occurs to what is potential or possible. Living organisms can then be better understood as dynamical systems. They self-regulate in order to fit their environment under two opposing types of constraints:

1. selective constraints, which reduce options;
2. enabling constraints, which provide options.

Reference: *Dynamics in Action: Intentional Behavior as a Complex System* — Alicia Juarrero, 1999.

> "Thinking of causes as dynamical constraints makes bottom-up and top-down causal relations, including those involving intentional causes, suddenly tractable."

**Fittedness and growth.** Just as evolution seeks fittedness under the opposing pressures of selection and diversification, living organisms grow by regulating fittedness within their own network of constraints. Arousal, for example, emerges from the opponent processes of the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems, which respectively prepare us for stress and for rest.

**Character as regulated constraint.** Applied to character, Aristotle's view suggests that foolishness, doing the wrong thing despite knowing better, stems from weaknesses in character. In modern terms, cultivation of character can be expressed as the regulation of dynamical systems of constraints. The constraints we place ourselves under, consciously or not, are decisive for personal growth. Courage, for example, emerges from the right balance between avoiding cowardice and avoiding recklessness.

## Getting in Touch with Reality: The Agent-Arena Relationship and the Cosmos

*Episode 7: A good fit between how we define ourselves and how we perceive the world grounds closer contact with reality*

**The agent-arena relationship.** This relationship involves an agent capable of pursuing goals and an arena, the environment that enables the agent. A stadium enables the football player, while the player gives significance to the stadium. We are always engaged or enmeshed in such relationships, perceiving our environment as a particular arena in which we take on particular roles.

**Worldview attunement.** When this process functions well, it contributes to a coherent worldview and a sense of belonging. When it fails, when there is no proper attunement between self and world, things appear absurd, like a tennis player in a football stadium.

**The nomological order.** At the cultural level, John Vervaeke calls a worldview that gives us a sense of fitting in and being connected to the world the *nomological order*. This sense of connectedness plays a major role in bringing us into closer touch with reality.

**The Greek cosmos.** The Greek cosmos is a strong example of such a worldview: an ordered whole in which everything has a proper place, and therefore a way of fitting and finding meaning.

Reference: *The Embodied Mind: Cognitive Science and Human Experience* — Francisco Varela, Eleanor Rosch, and Evan Thompson, 1992 (first edition), 2017 (revised edition).

> "The Embodied Mind introduced a new form of cognitive science called enaction, in which both the environment and first-person experience are aspects of embodiment."
